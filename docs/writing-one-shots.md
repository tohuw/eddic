# Writing a one-shot — situations, not stories

Advice for the DM who writes well and wants to author their own
material instead of running a boxed adventure. The instinct is sound:
published adventures are worst at exactly what a writer does for
free — evocative rooms, NPCs with voice, a villain worth hating. But
the craft transfers with one correction, and a new DM who absorbs it
early skips the most common first-campaign wound. Agents helping a DM
prep material should apply this doc's frame; the division-of-labor
section at the end is addressed to them as much as to the DM.

## Pilot, not campaign

Do not start a campaign. Write one self-contained adventure with an
explicit finish line, run it once, and see what happens. If it lands,
it retroactively becomes session one; if it doesn't, nothing is lost
and everything is learned. A pilot is also an easier ask for busy
players than an open-ended commitment.

Let the table's shortest attention span set the session length, and
design the arc to fit it — for mixed-age tables that is often ninety
minutes, not four hours. Put dice in hands inside the first fifteen
minutes, and end on time even mid-scene. Short and frequent beats
long and rare, for the players and for a DM still learning pace.

## The correction: a one-shot is not a story

Prose works because the author controls the protagonist. At the table
the protagonists are the players, and the moment the material *needs*
them to walk through the north door, someone will lick the south one.
A DM who writes a plot spends the session dragging players back onto
it, and everyone feels the drag. The fix is not to write less — it is
to point the writing at different targets:

- **Write the villain's plan, not the players' path.** What happens
  if nobody interferes? That timeline is the spine. The players
  collide with it wherever they like, and the material survives any
  angle of collision.
- **Write NPCs as wants, not scenes.** "The ferryman wants the thing
  under the Sunken City left undisturbed and will lie about why"
  survives any player behavior. "The ferryman tearfully confesses in
  act two" does not.
- **Write the ending as a question, not an outcome.** The author
  knows what happens if the Warden's seal breaks; the author
  genuinely does not know whether the players will stop it, join it,
  or bargain with it. If the ending is already known, it is a novel.

In short: write a place where something is about to go wrong, not a
tale of what happens.

## Scaffold: five scenes, one decision each

The five-room-dungeon shape holds a one-shot together regardless of
genre, and the "rooms" need not be literal rooms: entrance/guardian →
puzzle or social scene → setback/twist → climax → reward/revelation.
Five scenes, each built around one clear decision, fills ninety
minutes. The structure serves a mixed table: players who need speed
charge in, players who savor narrative get their flavor, players who
chew on problems get one meaty decision per scene — and open-ended
dead air, where mixed tables die, never gets room to open up.

Two more constraints that protect a first outing: keep the scope to
one location cluster, and hand out pregenerated characters. Character
creation is a full session by itself and the classic attention-span
killer; picking and renaming a ready-made character takes minutes,
and "build your own" becomes the hook for session two.

## The rules pass is the cheap half

New DMs overestimate the mechanical work. Monster stat blocks are
costumes-optional: a grief-hollowed shepherd whose shadow hunts
separately from his body is a wolf stat block plus a shadow stat
block wearing the fiction. This is reskinning, and it is how
experienced DMs work too. Everything else maps the same way — a
puzzle-door is a DC 13 Intelligence check, a rotten causeway a DC 10
Dexterity save. A finished draft backmaps to legal mechanics in about
an hour.

That hour is agent work. The division of labor: the human writes the
situation — the villain's plan, the wants, the rooms; the agent maps
it to stat blocks and DCs from the rules in play, checks encounter
math against the party, and flags any scene whose resolution the
draft has already decided (see above — that is the one thing the
agent should push back on, not silently fix). Respect authorship
throughout: the agent tunes numbers, never prose.

## Prep by reading, not writing

Before running: read the draft twice, read the combat chapter once.
The skill that matters on day one is not rules mastery but pace —
keep turns short, rule ambiguities instantly in the players' favor,
never stop the game to look something up. A wrong ruling costs
nothing; a ten-minute rules lookup costs the table.
