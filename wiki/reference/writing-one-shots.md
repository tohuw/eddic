# Writing one-shots

Advice for the DM who writes well and wants to author their own material
instead of running a boxed adventure. The instinct is sound: published
adventures are worst at exactly what a writer does for free — evocative
rooms, NPCs with a voice, a villain worth hating. But that craft only
transfers with one correction, and a new DM who absorbs the correction
early skips the most common first-campaign wound. Agents helping a DM prep
material should apply this doc's frame; the division-of-labor section at the
end is addressed as much to the agent as to the DM.

## Pilot, not campaign

Do not start a campaign. Write one self-contained adventure with an explicit
finish line, run it once, and see what happens. If it lands, it
retroactively becomes session one; if it doesn't, nothing is lost and
everything is learned. A pilot is also easier to ask of busy players than an
open-ended commitment.

Let the table's shortest attention span set the session length, and design
the arc to fit it — mixed-age tables often run ninety minutes, not four
hours. Put dice in hands inside the first fifteen minutes, and end on time
even mid-scene. Short and frequent beats long and rare while the players and
the DM are still learning the pace.

## The correction: a one-shot is not a story

Prose works because the author controls the protagonist. At the table the
protagonists are the players, and the moment the material *needs* them to
walk through the north door, someone will lick the south one. A DM who
writes a plot spends the session dragging players back onto it, and everyone
feels the drag. The fix is not to write less — it is to point the writing at
different targets:

- **Write the villain's plan, not the players' path.** What happens if
  nobody interferes? That timeline is the spine. The players collide with it
  wherever they like, and the material survives any angle of collision.
- **Write NPCs' wants, not scenes.** "The ferryman wants the thing under the
  Sunken City left undisturbed and will lie about why" survives any player
  behavior. "The ferryman tearfully confesses in act two" does not.
- **Write an ending question, not an outcome.** The author knows what
  happens if the Warden's seal breaks; the author genuinely does not know
  whether the players will stop it, join it, or bargain with it. If the
  ending is already known, it is a novel.

In short: write a place where something is about to go wrong, not a tale of
what happens.

## Scaffold: five scenes, one decision each

The five-room-dungeon shape holds a one-shot together regardless of genre,
and the "rooms" need not be literal rooms: entrance/guardian → puzzle or
social scene → setback/twist → climax → reward/revelation. Five scenes, each
built around one clear decision, fills ninety minutes. The structure serves
a mixed table: players who need speed charge in, players who savor narrative
get flavor, and players who chew on problems get one meaty decision per
scene — the open-ended dead air where mixed tables die never gets room to
open up.

Two more constraints protect a first outing: keep the scope to one location
cluster, and hand out pregenerated characters. Character creation is a full
session by itself and a classic attention-span killer; picking and renaming
a ready-made character takes minutes, and "build your own" becomes the hook
for session two.

## The rules pass is the cheap half

New DMs overestimate the mechanical work. Monster stat blocks are
costumes-optional: a grief-hollowed shepherd whose shadow hunts separately
from his body is a wolf stat block plus a shadow stat block wearing new
descriptions — which is how experienced DMs work too. Everything else maps
the same way — a puzzle-door is a DC 13 Intelligence check, a rotten
causeway a DC 10 Dexterity save. A finished draft backmaps to legal
mechanics in about an hour.

That hour is agent work. The division of labor: the human writes the
situation — the villain's plan, the wants, the rooms; the agent maps it to
stat blocks, DCs, and rules-in-play, checks the encounter math against the
party, and flags any scene whose resolution the draft has already decided
(see above — the one thing the agent should push back on, not silently fix).
This is the [deterministic core, agent shell](../concepts/deterministic-core.md)
split in miniature: judgment writes the situation, mechanical work maps it.
Respect authorship throughout: the agent tunes numbers, never prose — the
[authorship-preservation principle](../design/principles.md) binds here as
everywhere.

## Prep by reading, not writing

Before running: read the draft twice, and read the combat chapter once. The
skill that matters on day one is not rules mastery but pace — keep turns
short, rule ambiguities instantly in the players' favor, and never stop the
game to look something up. A wrong ruling costs nothing; a ten-minute rules
lookup costs the table.
